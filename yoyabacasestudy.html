üìã Executive Summary

This document outlines a strategic approach to evaluating and implementing a major CRM (Salesforce or HubSpot) for a 200-employee B2B SaaS company with an outdated, partly self-built GoToMarket architecture.

The analysis reframes this initiative not as a simple CRM replacement project, but as a comprehensive GTM architecture normalization effort that establishes clear system boundaries, data ownership, and integration principles.

üí° Key Insight:

The goal is not to choose the "best" CRM, but to redefine system boundaries so that the CRM becomes a stable orchestration layer rather than another monolith.

üìù Explicit Assumptions
  • The company operates a hybrid GTM motion (marketing-led acquisition with sales-assisted conversion)
  • The current CRM (Bitrix24) is functionally overloaded, handling sales, partner management, CS, task management, and calling
  • GTM processes are operationally active but architecturally misaligned
  • The Admin backend is custom-built and connected to MySQL
üí≠ First Thoughts

üî¨ 1a. Free Trial vs Demo ‚Äì Lead to Customer Conversion
‚ùì Question:

Is a prospect company more likely to convert from Lead to Customer if they first have a free trial or first have a demo?

Analytical Approach:

  • Identify first recorded CTA per company (first demo booked date, first free trial signup date)
  • Attribute company to Demo-first or Trial-first
  • Compare Lead ‚Üí Customer conversion rates at company level

Key Assumptions:

  • CTA choice reflects buyer self-selection and intent
  • Demo-first companies typically show higher readiness to buy
  • Trial-first companies include earlier-stage and exploratory interest
üìä Finding:

Demo-first companies are more likely to convert from Lead to Customer than trial-first companies.

Interpretation: The difference is driven by buyer intent, not inherent superiority of demos. Trial-first generates scale but lower conversion efficiency.

CONCLUSION

  • Demo-first = higher conversion efficiency
  • Trial-first = broader top-of-funnel volume
  • Optimal GTM requires differentiated routing, not choosing one motion
üìà 1b. Weekly Cohort Evaluation ‚Äì SQL Conversion Timing
‚ùì Question:

Given cohort week X, after how many weeks (X + n) should week X be fully evaluated, assuming 90% of all leads that convert to SQL should have already converted?

Why This Matters:

  • SQL conversions are time-distributed, not instantaneous
  • Evaluating cohorts too early understates performance
  • Evaluating too late slows experimentation and decision-making
üéØ Reference Population

To learn typical conversion timing, we analyze a stable historical segment:

  • 582 companies
  • 50+ employees
  • Created between 2020‚Äì2023
  • That eventually converted to SQL

This ensures sufficient sample size, mature conversion data, and comparable buying behavior.

üîç Finding
üìä Finding:

The 90% threshold is reached at approximately 12 weeks (~85 days). After week 12, additional SQL conversions occur only marginally, indicating that the conversion curve has largely flattened.

‚úÖ Conclusion

CONCLUSION

  • A cohort week X should be considered fully evaluated after X + 12 weeks
  • This captures the vast majority of SQL signal
  • While avoiding premature or delayed evaluation

Although the exact 90th percentile occurs slightly after week 12, using a 12-week evaluation window provides a robust and operationally practical standard for cohort analysis.

üéØ Key Takeaway:

Weekly lead cohorts should be evaluated at X + 12 weeks to capture ~90% of all SQL conversions and enable confident, timely GTM decisions.
